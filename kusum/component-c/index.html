Component C is for farmers who ALREADY have a grid-connected electric pump. You add solar panels sized to match your pump's load. During the day, you use solar power preferentially — and any excess goes back to the DISCOM at ₹3.50 per unit. Daytime grid load drops to zero; nighttime backup is still available from the grid.

§ Two sub-modes within Component C

C1 vs C2: which applies to you?

Component C has two sub-modes. C1 is for individual farmers solarising a single pump; C2 is for entire feeders (multiple farmers in a village sharing a feeder-level solar plant). Most UP farmers get C1.

C1 · INDIVIDUAL PUMP SOLARISATION

Most common path

Solar panels installed for YOUR specific grid-connected pump. The setup is yours alone — you control it, you benefit from the savings, you keep the excess-generation income.

Applies to: Individual farmer with a metered electricity connection on the agricultural pump.

C2 · FEEDER-LEVEL SOLARISATION

Village or feeder-level

A larger solar plant solarises the entire agricultural feeder serving multiple farmers. The benefit is distributed across all farmers on that feeder. Implemented by the DISCOM, not by individual farmers.

Applies to: Villages or feeder zones where DISCOM proposes feeder-level solar (consent required from all farmers on that feeder).

If you're applying as an individual, you're almost certainly looking at C1. The rest of this page focuses on C1.

§ Who Component C1 is built for

Best fit: existing grid-pump farmers.

Component C1 only works if you already have an electric pump connection to the grid. If you're rain-fed or diesel-pumping, Component B (standalone) is your path.

✅ Strong fit if you have

  • Existing grid-connected agricultural pump (1-10 HP)
  • Active electricity bill ≥ ₹500/month (means you're actually using the pump)
  • Active pump usage at least 4 months per year
  • At least 0.5 acres of cultivable agricultural land
  • Valid land records + active electricity account in your name
  • Can pay ₹15K-65K own funds within 30 days of approval

❌ Probably NOT for you if

  • No grid-connected pump yet (Component B applies instead)
  • Existing pump bill is < ₹500/month (math doesn't justify investment)
  • Existing pump motor is > 10 years old (needs replacement first)
  • DISCOM hasn't approved net metering for agricultural connections in your area
  • Less than 0.5 acres of land
  • Pump is jointly registered with multiple owners (paperwork complexity)

7.5 HP example (for comparison)

If you have a 7.5 HP pump, solar sizing scales up to ~9 kWp, total cost ₹4,83,750. Your share is ₹1,93,500 (₹65K own funds + ₹1.45L loan). Annual benefit ~₹45,900. Payback still ~4.2 years.

The economics are remarkably consistent across pump sizes — payback is roughly 4-5 years regardless of HP, because the solar is sized proportionally to the pump.

§ Application process

From application to active net metering.

Typical timeline: 4 months. Faster than Component A; similar to Component B. The DISCOM net-metering step adds some complexity — that's where most delays occur.

Step 01 · Day 1

Eligibility + Pump Audit

Free eligibility check + vendor audits your existing pump (age, condition, motor type) to confirm solarisation feasibility.

Step 02 · Day 7-30

DISCOM Feasibility

DISCOM reviews your existing electricity connection + verifies net-metering eligibility on agricultural feeders in your area.

Step 03 · Day 30-60

UPNEDA Application

Submit application with existing electricity bill + pump connection number + land records.

Step 04 · Day 60-90

Solar Install + Meter Replace

Vendor installs solar panels + connects to existing pump system. DISCOM replaces your standard meter with bi-directional (net) meter.

Step 05 · Day 90-120

Commissioning + First Credit

Net metering activated. First bill cycle shows solar generation + excess export credit. From here on, monthly bills are net of solar contribution.

§ Component C FAQ

Common questions, direct answers.

Do I keep my grid pump connection?

Yes. Component C ADDS solar to your existing pump — it does not replace the grid connection. During the day, you use solar power preferentially (with grid as backup if solar isn't sufficient). At night, the pump runs purely on grid. The grid is your safety net for cloudy days, monsoon, or nighttime irrigation.

How is solar sizing determined?

Solar capacity is sized at roughly 1.2× pump HP in kWp. So 5 HP pump → 6 kWp solar; 7.5 HP → 9 kWp; 10 HP → 12 kWp. The 1.2× factor allows for some excess generation that can be sold to DISCOM.

What's the excess export tariff in UP?

UP DISCOMs (UPPCL, MVVNL, DVVNL, PuVVNL, PvVNL, KESCO) currently pay ₹3.50/unit for excess solar exported from Component C systems. This is set by UPNEDA + UPERC (UP Electricity Regulatory Commission). The tariff is locked at the rate effective on your commissioning date and remains valid for the life of your system (typically 25 years).

What's a bi-directional meter? Do I have to pay for it?

A bi-directional (or "net") meter measures both energy you consume from the grid AND energy you export to the grid. Your monthly bill is calculated as net consumption (consumption minus export). The DISCOM provides and installs the meter FREE as part of Component C — you don't pay separately for it. Old single-direction meters are removed.

How long until I see savings on my bill?

From the day net metering is commissioned. Your next monthly bill will reflect solar generation. If solar fully offsets your usage in a given month, your bill will be ₹0 (or a tiny fixed-charge minimum). If you have excess, the credit is carried forward to subsequent months. Most farmers see 70-90% bill reduction within the first month.

What if my pump usage is seasonal?

Component C works especially well for seasonal farmers. During non-irrigation months (monsoon, winter fallow), almost all your solar generation becomes excess and is sold to DISCOM. So you earn income even when you're not actively farming. UPPCL records show Component C farmers earning ₹10K-20K per year from excess sales during off-season.

What about transformer / line capacity issues?

Some agricultural feeders have transformer capacity issues that prevent net metering. UPNEDA + DISCOM will check feasibility before approval. If your feeder is overloaded, you may be asked to wait until a transformer upgrade (which DISCOM does on its own timeline) or be redirected to Component B (standalone). This is uncommon but happens in densely-pumped areas like parts of Western UP.

Can I use Component C if my pump is on a leased land?

You need land ownership for the application. If the pump itself is registered in your name but the land is leased, you'll need a No-Objection Certificate (NOC) from the landowner. Most landowners agree readily because solar improves the land's value. But it's a paperwork step. Owned land is simpler.

What happens if my pump motor breaks down?

The pump motor is your responsibility (not covered under the solar AMC). If it breaks down, you replace it at your cost. The solar panels keep generating regardless. For Component C, your pump motor is the "weak link" — if it's already old, consider replacing it before applying for Component C (so you don't end up with a fresh solar system attached to a dying motor).

Can I switch from Component C to B later?

If you later want to go fully off-grid (disconnect from the DISCOM), it's possible but requires re-applying under Component B with a fresh installation. The Component C investment isn't transferable. So pick your path carefully — once committed to C, you typically stay with C.

What if my electricity bill is highly variable?

Component C math works best for steady consumption (₹800-3000/month). If your bills swing wildly (e.g., ₹200 some months, ₹4,000 others), the average matters more than the variance. As long as your annual electricity cost exceeds ₹6,000, Component C typically pays back within 5 years. Below that, the savings don't justify the investment — consider Component B instead.

Do I need to maintain the bi-directional meter?

No — it's DISCOM property. They maintain it. You're just a user. If the meter malfunctions, you call your local DISCOM office. Repairs are free. Don't tamper with it; meter tampering carries penalties under the Electricity Act 2003.
