§ What is Component C?

How Component C works.

Component C adds a solar array to your existing grid-connected agricultural pump. The pump runs primarily on solar power during the day. Any excess generation gets fed back into the UPPCL grid via a bi-directional meter, and you get paid for it.

Unlike Component B (which is for off-grid farms), Component C requires that you already have an electricity connection servicing your pump. The solar array doesn't replace the grid — it supplements it. When the sun is shining, the pump uses solar. When it's not, the pump uses grid power. Excess solar generation (typically 40% of total output) flows back to UPPCL and earns you ₹3.50 per unit at the feed-in tariff.

This makes Component C two income streams in one: (1) lower electricity bills because you're using less grid power, and (2) export income from excess generation. Combined annual benefit on a 5 HP pump: ~₹30,000+ per year.

✅ Why this beats Component B for grid-connected farmers

If you already have a working electric pump, replacing it with Component B's standalone solar pump is wasteful — you'd be paying for new pump hardware you don't need. Component C reuses your existing pump infrastructure and just adds solar panels. Lower cost, faster payback, and you keep grid backup for cloudy days.

§ Two sub-components

C1 vs C2 — which one applies?

Component C has two delivery models. Most farmers want C1.

C1 — Individual

Individual Pump Solarisation

You install solar panels on your own farm, sized to your specific pump. Bi-directional meter measures excess export. Tariff credited monthly to your bank account.

Solar capacity: typically 1.2× your pump HP in kWp. So a 5 HP pump gets ~6 kWp solar. A 7.5 HP pump gets ~9 kWp.

This is the path most farmers want — full control, direct savings, individual ownership.

For: individual farmers with their own pump connection
C2 — Feeder-Level

Feeder-Level Solarisation

UPPCL solarises an entire agricultural feeder (the distribution line serving multiple farmers). Solar generation is shared across all farmers on that feeder. Each farmer gets reduced tariffs on grid usage.

No equipment on individual farms. Instead, a community-scale plant serves the entire feeder.

Usually implemented by DISCOM with panchayat consent. Individual farmers don't apply directly — it's a village-level initiative.

For: villages where DISCOM has identified a high-loss agricultural feeder

When you use the eligibility check, we route you to C1 by default since that's the path individual farmers actually apply for.

§ Eligibility

Do you qualify?

✅ Strong fit

  • You have an existing UPPCL agricultural pump connection (with consumption history)
  • Your pump is 1–10 HP capacity
  • Your pump is actively used for at least 4 months per year
  • You own the agricultural land (RoR / Khasra-Khatauni in your name)
  • Your DISCOM circle has approved net metering for agricultural connections
  • Your existing pump motor is less than 10 years old (older motors may need replacement first)

⛔ Not a fit

  • You don't have an existing grid-connected pump (use Component B for off-grid)
  • Your monthly pump bill is under ₹500 — the math may not work out
  • Your existing pump motor is more than 10 years old (replacement required first)
  • You're a tenant farmer without ownership documents
  • Your DISCOM hasn't approved net metering for agricultural connections in your circle
  • Your pump usage is highly seasonal (less than 4 months/year)

§ The process

From application to net metering.

Typical timeline: ~4 months from application to bi-directional meter activation. Similar to Component B but adds the DISCOM net-metering approval step.

Step 01 · Day 1-7

Apply on UPNEDA portal

Submit land records, Aadhaar, existing pump connection number, recent electricity bills (last 3 months).

Step 02 · Day 7-30

DISCOM feasibility

DISCOM verifies pump existence + consumption history. Confirms net metering approval for your circle. Pump motor condition checked.

Step 03 · Day 30-75

Vendor + sizing

MNRE-empanelled vendor assigned. Site visit. Solar array sized to match pump load. Subsidy sanction issued.

Step 04 · Day 75-105

Installation

Vendor installs solar panels, inverter, cabling, integration with existing pump circuit.

Step 05 · Day 105-120

Bi-directional meter

DISCOM installs bi-directional meter (free). Commissioning. Export earnings credited monthly thereafter.

§ Red flags

What to watch for.

  • DISCOM hasn't approved net metering for agricultural connections. Some UPPCL circles (rural belts in eastern UP) still don't have net metering enabled for agricultural pumps. Without it, you can't sell excess to the grid — destroying half the economics. Verify your circle status before applying.
  • Vendor proposes oversized solar array for kickback. A 5 HP pump shouldn't get 10 kWp solar — that's 2× overcapacity. Excess capacity gets capped by net metering rules. Some vendors push oversize because their commission is higher; the math doesn't work for you.
  • Existing pump motor is old (10+ years). KUSUM-C requires functional pump motor. If your motor is at end-of-life, vendor will require replacement BEFORE solarisation. Don't let vendor hide this — get it inspected upfront.
  • "Excess generation will go to your neighbour." No — that's Component C2 (feeder-level). In C1, excess goes via bi-directional meter to UPPCL, credited to your account. Vendor or DISCOM that tells you otherwise is misinforming you.
  • Feeder-level (C2) proposed without panchayat consent. If a vendor or DISCOM employee approaches you about "feeder solarisation" without showing village-level panchayat resolution, walk away. C2 requires community consent.
