§ What it actually is

Component C in plain language.

You have an electric pump on your agricultural land. It's connected to UPPCL via a metered connection, and you pay a monthly electricity bill (typically ₹500–3,000+ depending on usage). You like the grid pump because it works reliably, but the bills are a real cost.

Component C lets you install a solar panel array sized to your pump (rule of thumb: kWp = pump HP × 1.2). The pump now runs on solar power during the day. Excess solar generation — generated when the pump isn't drawing maximum power, or on holidays when you're not irrigating — is exported back to the grid via a bi-directional meter. UPPCL credits you ₹3.50 per unit of excess generation.

Same subsidy structure as Component B: 30% MNRE + 30% UPNEDA + 40% farmer share (of which 30% bank loan eligible). Your minimum out-of-pocket: 10% of total cost.

§ Two sub-components

C1 vs C2 — which one are you?

Component C has two sub-variants. Most individual farmers fall under C1.

Component C1

Individual Pump Solarisation

This is what most farmers want. You install solar capacity to power your own pump. You're the only customer of this solar array. Excess generation sold to UPPCL.

Best for: individual farmers with 0.5–7 acres and a working grid-connected pump (1–10 HP).

Component C2

Feeder-Level Solarisation

A solar plant solarises an entire agricultural feeder serving multiple farmers. Implemented by the DISCOM (UPPCL) at the feeder level, with consent from all farmers on that feeder.

Best for: village-level coordination through panchayat. Individual farmers can't apply directly — requires DISCOM-led initiative.

The rest of this page focuses on Component C1 — the individual-pump path that most UP farmers will pursue.

§ Eligibility (C1)

Who qualifies for Component C1?

✅ You qualify if:

  • You own at least 0.5 acres of agricultural land
  • You have a grid-connected electric pump on the land (1–10 HP)
  • The pump has its own electricity meter (not unmetered flat-rate)
  • Your monthly pump bill is at least ₹500
  • You actively use the pump at least 4 months per year
  • Your land has space for the solar panel array (roughly 90–110 sq ft per kWp)

⚠️ You don't qualify if:

  • You don't currently have a grid-connected pump (Component B fits instead)
  • Your pump runs on unmetered flat-rate (you'd need to install a meter first — complex)
  • Your monthly bill is below ₹500 (the math may not work — payback too slow)
  • Your land doesn't have space for solar panels (rooftop on a farmhouse won't power a borewell efficiently)
  • You've already received KUSUM-C subsidy in the past 5 years
Watch the bill threshold: Component C makes financial sense above ₹500/month in pump electricity bills. Below that, the system cost takes too long to pay back — you'd be installing solar to save money that isn't really being spent. The eligibility check has this threshold built in.

§ Application process

From application to operational solar pump.

Typical timeline: 4 months from application to commissioning. The DISCOM (UPPCL) feasibility step is the main differentiator from Component B.

Apply on UPNEDA portalOnline application with your existing pump connection number. Land records, Aadhaar, bank account, recent electricity bill (to verify the connection).
DISCOM verification + feasibility (30–45 days)UPPCL verifies your pump connection exists, checks consumption history, confirms feasibility for net metering on the agricultural feeder.
Vendor selection + solar sizingUPNEDA-empanelled vendor surveys your land, recommends panel placement, confirms kWp size based on pump HP.
Pay your 40% shareSubsidy is netted; you pay the vendor your share. Bank loan applied for and disbursed if needed.
Installation (15–30 days)Vendor installs solar panels, mounting structure, wiring to existing pump.

§ Common questions

Component C specifics.

Does the solar array power my home too, or just the pump?

Under Component C, the solar array is tied specifically to your agricultural pump connection. The export credit applies only to that connection's bill. If you want rooftop solar for your home, that's PM Surya Ghar — a separate scheme on your residential connection. You can have both independently.

What happens to my existing pump motor?

Your existing electric pump motor stays as-is. The solar array feeds power into the same circuit. On cloudy days or at night (if you irrigate then), the pump draws from the grid as usual.

Will my pump work during a power outage?

Yes, during daytime hours when the solar is generating. The pump can run on solar even when the grid is down (the system is grid-tied with anti-islanding, so it disconnects from the grid during outages but continues to power the pump directly). This is a hidden benefit of Component C for areas with frequent UP power cuts.

How long do the solar panels last?

Solar panels are warrantied for 25 years (linear degradation, typically 80% output retained at year 25). The MPPT/inverter electronics are warrantied for 5 years (extendable). The 5-year comprehensive AMC covers all of this for the first 5 years.

Can I add more solar capacity later?

Yes, but the subsidy is one-time. You'd pay full cost for any additional capacity added later. So size it correctly the first time — the eligibility check helps with this.
