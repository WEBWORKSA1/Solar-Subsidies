§ Component C has two sub-paths

Two ways to solarise.

Component C has two implementation models. C1 is for individual farmer pumps; C2 is for whole agricultural feeders shared by multiple farmers. Most individual applications are C1.

Sub-component C1

Individual Pump Solarisation

Solar panels installed for your specific grid-connected pump. Your pump uses solar preferentially during the day; excess generation goes to the DISCOM via a bi-directional meter. This is the most common Component C application.

You apply individually. DISCOM installs the bi-directional meter. Excess sells at ₹3.50/unit.

Sub-component C2

Feeder-Level Solarisation

A solar plant solarises an entire agricultural feeder serving multiple farmers in a village. Implemented by the DISCOM with consent from the farmers connected to that feeder. Reduces DISCOM peak load while keeping farmer tariffs unchanged.

Initiated by DISCOM, not individual farmer. Requires village-level consent.

For most individual UP farmers reading this page, C1 is what you'll apply for. The rest of this page focuses on C1. If you're a panchayat or FPO interested in C2 feeder solarisation, contact your local UPNEDA district office directly.

§ Honest fit assessment

Is Component C right for you?

Component C only works if you already have a grid-connected pump AND you actually use it enough for solar to pay back. Low-usage farms see weak ROI. High-bill farms see strong returns.

✅ Strong fit if:

  • You have an existing grid-connected electric pump on agricultural land
  • Your monthly electricity bill for the pump is ₹500+ (this is the threshold for solar to pay back)
  • You use the pump 4+ months per year (sufficient solar generation to capture)
  • Your DISCOM has a net metering policy for agricultural connections (UPPCL does, MVVNL/PuVVNL/etc all do)
  • You own the land where the pump is installed
  • You can fund 10% own funds upfront (₹13K-₹68K depending on solar capacity)

⚠️ Not a fit if:

  • You don't have an existing grid pump — Component B (off-grid) is your path, not C
  • Your monthly pump bill is under ₹500 — payback period stretches to 8+ years; not worth the capex
  • You use the pump less than 3 months/year — solar generates surplus you can't use, and excess-sale alone won't pay back
  • Your pump motor is over 10 years old — replace it first, then solarise
  • You're a tenant farmer on the land — Component C requires ownership documents

Why C is sometimes better than B for grid-connected farmers

If you already have a grid pump and were thinking about replacing it with a Component B solar pump, run the numbers. Component C lets you keep using your existing pump (and grid power as backup during cloudy days/early morning) while adding solar that pays back in ~4 years AND keeps generating income for 20+ years after that.

Component B replaces your pump entirely (one-time benefit). Component C augments it (ongoing dual benefit). For most farmers with reliable grid connections, C is the smarter math.

§ Application process

From application to net meter active.

Total timeline: ~4 months from initial application to operational solar with net metering. Faster than Component A (which involves plant construction), similar timeline to Component B.

Step 01 · Day 1-7

Apply with Pump Details

Apply on UPNEDA portal with your existing pump connection number, electricity bill, land records.

Step 02 · Day 7-30

DISCOM Feasibility Check

UPPCL verifies pump existence + consumption history. Confirms net metering eligibility for your connection.

Step 03 · Day 30-60

Vendor Empanelment + Sizing

UPNEDA-empanelled vendor assesses your site, sizes the solar array (typically 1.2× pump HP in kWp).

Step 04 · Day 60-105

Installation + Net Meter

Vendor installs solar array (15-30 days). DISCOM installs bi-directional meter (10-15 days post-installation).

Step 05 · Day 105-120

Commissioning

System commissioned, net metering active. Excess generation credited monthly to your DISCOM bill.

§ Watch out for

Component C red flags.

Component C is technically more complex than B because it involves grid integration. The vendor needs both solar EPC skills AND net-metering paperwork experience. Choose carefully.

🚩 Refuse to sign if:

  • Vendor doesn't mention bi-directional meter installation by DISCOM as part of the process — without this, you can't export excess generation
  • Vendor sizes the solar array at exactly your pump HP in kWp instead of 1.2× — undersizing kills the excess-export economics
  • Vendor claims you can still draw subsidised agricultural tariff on the grid side after solarisation — UP rules don't permit this, your tariff slab may change
  • Vendor is not on the UPNEDA empanelled list for Component C specifically (some vendors are empanelled only for B, not C)
  • Pump itself is over 10 years old and vendor doesn't recommend replacement first — solarising a failing pump is throwing good money after bad
  • No written guarantee of net-meter installation timeline from DISCOM (without the meter, excess generation gets wasted)
§ Common questions

Component C FAQ.

What happens to my existing electricity connection — do I keep paying bills?

Yes, you keep the grid connection. The solar reduces how much grid power you use (typically by 50-60%), so your bills shrink significantly. You also receive credits for excess solar generation exported to the grid. Some months you may have a near-zero bill or even a credit. The grid acts as backup for cloudy days and early-morning irrigation before solar kicks in.

What's the difference between net metering and gross metering?

Net metering: your meter spins backward when you export solar excess, and your bill is calculated on net consumption (grid imports minus solar exports). This is what UP offers under Component C. Gross metering pays you separately for all solar generation while you pay full retail for grid imports — generally less favourable but used in some states. UP's net metering rate is ₹3.50/unit for agricultural excess, which is a feed-in tariff.

What if my pump is on a single-phase connection?

Single-phase pumps up to 3 HP are fine. Above 3 HP, agricultural pumps are typically three-phase. If you have a 5+ HP pump, it's almost certainly three-phase already, which is what Component C is designed for. Single-phase systems require simpler solar setups (no three-phase inverter needed) but follow the same subsidy math.

How much will my electricity bill actually drop?

Typical drop: 50-65% in the first year after net metering goes live. Why not 100%? Because solar generates during daylight only, but agricultural pumps sometimes run early morning (before sunrise) or late evening (after sunset) when farmers prefer reduced wind/evaporation. The grid covers those hours. The 35-50% remaining bill is from those non-solar-hours.

Example: If your pre-solar bill was ₹2,500/month, expect ₹900-₹1,200/month post-solar. Plus you'll see export credits when you don't use the pump (monsoon, winter fallow).

What if my excess generation exceeds my consumption for the year?

Annual credit settlement works as follows: at year-end (typically March 31), UPPCL settles any net positive balance (more exports than imports across the year) at the feed-in tariff of ₹3.50/unit, paid directly to your bank account. Most agricultural Component C installations end the year with a small positive balance — additional income of ₹2,000-₹8,000 depending on system size.

Do I need to maintain the solar panels?

Minimal maintenance: clean panels every 2-3 months during dry season (dust accumulation reduces output by 5-15%). The vendor's 5-year AMC covers panel inspection, inverter checkup, and any equipment failures. After 5 years, you're responsible for cleaning (which is a 30-minute job with water + soft cloth) and any inverter replacement (₹15,000-₹40,000 every 5-7 years).

Can I add battery backup to a Component C system?

Technically yes, but batteries are not subsidised under KUSUM. Adding batteries to a 6 kWp system would add ₹1.5-₹2.5 Lakh in cost (no subsidy) for the ability to run the pump at night or during outages. Most agricultural users don't need night-time pumping, so the ROI doesn't work. Skip the batteries unless you have a specific reason (e.g., aquaculture, dairy with continuous pumping needs).

What happens when I sell or transfer the land?

The solar system and pump transfer with the land (it's a fixture, not movable equipment). The net-metering connection is tied to the property's electricity connection number, which transfers to the new owner along with the deed. The new owner inherits the AMC (if still active) and the income stream. Disclose the Component C installation as a property feature — it typically increases land value by 4-6% in agricultural areas of UP.
