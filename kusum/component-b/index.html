§ Who Component B is built for

Best fit: rain-fed or diesel-pump farmers.

Component B works for any farmer with at least 0.5 acres who needs irrigation but doesn't have reliable grid electricity. Especially valuable in Bundelkhand and Terai regions of UP where power cuts during sowing season cripple crops.

✅ Strong fit if you have

  • At least 0.5 acres of cultivable agricultural land
  • An identifiable water source (borewell, well, pond, river, canal)
  • Currently using diesel pump, manual lift, or rain-fed only
  • OR grid pump but with unreliable electricity (frequent outages)
  • Valid land records (RoR / Khasra-Khatauni)
  • Can pay ₹10K-60K own funds within 30 days of approval (depending on pump size)

❌ Probably NOT for you if

  • You have a reliable grid-connected pump (Component C is better)
  • Less than 0.5 acres of land
  • No identifiable water source on your land
  • Already received KUSUM-B subsidy in the past 5 years
  • Tenant farmer without land ownership documents
  • You only need a pump for non-agricultural use (gardens, fish farming)

Compared to a diesel pump

If you're currently spending ₹15,000-30,000 per year on diesel for irrigation, the solar pump pays for itself in 3-5 years on diesel savings alone. After payback, the pump runs free for another 20+ years (typical solar panel lifespan).

§ Component B FAQ

Common questions, direct answers.

How do I choose the right pump HP for my land?

Rough rule: 1 HP per 0.5-1 acre. Add HP if you grow water-intensive crops (sugarcane, paddy) or have a deep water table (>100 ft). Our eligibility check does this calculation automatically based on your land size, primary crop, and water source depth. Most UP farmers end up with 3-5 HP pumps.

Does the pump work on cloudy days?

Reduced output but still works. Modern KUSUM-approved solar pumps include Variable Frequency Drives (VFDs) that adjust pump speed to available solar power. On a cloudy day at 30% irradiance, your pump runs at 30% capacity. On a peak sunny day, it runs at 100%. Pump only stops at night or in heavy rain/storms.

What about monsoon — do I still need the pump?

Monsoon waters your fields for free, so pump usage drops. But for crops planted before monsoon (April-June) or after (October-November), the pump is essential. Most KUSUM-B farmers run the pump May-July and October-March. The solar panels still generate during monsoon, but the unused energy just goes uncaptured (no battery storage in standalone systems).

Can the pump be moved to a different field?

Most KUSUM-B pumps are designed for fixed installation (panels permanently mounted, pump in fixed borewell). Some vendors offer mobile-mounted variants where the pump and panels can be transported between fields — useful for rotational cropping. Mobile variants cost 10-15% more but offer flexibility. Ask your vendor.

What's the warranty?

Mandatory 5-year comprehensive maintenance is bundled with installation — vendor covers all parts + labour for 5 years. Solar panels themselves come with 25-year manufacturer warranty (degrades to ~80% efficiency at year 25, still operational). Pump motor (BLDC) typically lasts 10-15 years before replacement. After year 5, you'll pay for repairs out-of-pocket.

Can I add a battery for nighttime irrigation?

Standard KUSUM-B does not include battery storage (would double the cost). Some vendors offer aftermarket battery additions, but the economics rarely make sense — most farmers irrigate during the day anyway. If you specifically need nighttime irrigation, Component C (grid-connected) is a better fit since you have grid backup at night.

What if my borewell goes dry?

The pump can't pump water that isn't there. If your borewell yield decreases over time (common in deep aquifer regions), you may need to deepen the borewell (KUSUM does NOT subsidize this — separate cost ₹30K-1L). Some farmers add a second borewell with the same solar setup. The panels can support either borewell, just not simultaneously.

Can I sell excess solar power to the grid?

Component B is OFF-GRID — no connection to DISCOM, so no, you can't sell excess. If you want to sell excess solar to DISCOM, that's Component C (grid-connected solar pump) instead. Component C requires you to already have a grid pump connection. If you only have rain-fed or diesel currently, Component B is the right path.

Who actually owns the pump?

You own it. The subsidy goes to the vendor (not you), and you pay your 40% share. After your share is paid, the pump is your asset. The vendor's only ongoing obligation is the 5-year AMC (maintenance). After 5 years, the pump is fully yours with no obligations. You can sell it if you want (though resale market is limited).

What if the vendor goes out of business during the 5-year warranty?

UPNEDA maintains a list of empanelled backup-vendors who can take over warranty obligations if your original vendor exits the market. In practice, this is rare for MNRE-empanelled players (they're vetted). But it's worth asking your vendor about this scenario before signing. A reputable vendor will have written contingency clauses in the AMC contract.

Can I get this if I'm not a UP resident but own land in UP?

Yes. KUSUM applications are tied to the LAND, not the applicant's residence. NRIs and out-of-state landowners can apply if they own UP agricultural land with valid records. You'll need an Indian bank account (NABARD-channelled bank) for subsidy disbursement and to send the farmer-share payment. Some UPNEDA offices may require a local contact person on file.
