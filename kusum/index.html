§ Honest assessment

Should you apply for KUSUM?

Most KUSUM blog content is written by vendors trying to sell you pumps. We're not. Here's who actually benefits, and who shouldn't bother.

✅ Strong fit if:

  • You own at least 0.5 acres of agricultural land
  • You have an identifiable water source (borewell, well, pond, or canal access)
  • Your current irrigation is rain-fed, diesel pump, or unreliable grid electricity
  • You have valid land records (RoR / Khasra-Khatauni) and an Aadhaar-linked bank account
  • You can put up the 10% own-funds portion (₹10K-₹60K depending on pump size) within 30 days of approval

⚠️ Probably not a fit if:

  • You're a tenant farmer without ownership of the land (KUSUM requires land ownership documents)
  • You already have reliable grid electricity for your pump (Component C might help but check carefully — sometimes the math doesn't work)
  • You can't identify a stable water source on your land
  • You already received a KUSUM subsidy in the past 5 years (anti-double-subsidy rule applies)

Why KUSUM is different from rooftop solar

PM Surya Ghar (rooftop) gets all the marketing attention because urban customers have higher willingness to pay. KUSUM is structurally more valuable for the right farmer — 60% subsidy vs ~40% on rooftop, with bank loan eligibility for another 30%. But the eligibility checks are different, vendor pool is different, and most agents don't understand the three components.

Our calculator handles all three. Component A (large land), Component B (off-grid pump), Component C (grid-connected pump). Different qualifying questions, different subsidy math, different timelines.

§ Documents you'll need

What to keep ready.

None of these need to be ready today — you can start the eligibility check without any documents. Gather these once you confirm you're applying.

  • Aadhaar (farmer + spouse if married)
  • Land records — RoR / Khasra-Khatauni for UP, proving ownership of the agricultural land
  • Bank account proof — NABARD-channelled bank preferred (PNB, SBI, BoB, Canara Bank). Account must be Aadhaar-linked.
  • Water source proof — borewell certificate from drilling company, or GPS photograph of well/pond
  • Recent electricity bill (Component C only — to verify existing grid connection)
  • Photograph of installation site
  • Caste certificate (if SC/ST applying for reserved quota slot)
§ KUSUM FAQ for UP farmers

Common questions, direct answers.

I'm a tenant farmer — can I apply for KUSUM?

Unfortunately no. KUSUM Components A, B, and C all require land ownership documents. Tenant farmers cannot apply directly. You can try to get the landowner to apply (the pump stays with the land), or consider partnership models. Some states have lease-based KUSUM variants — UP does not yet.

Can I get KUSUM AND PM Surya Ghar on the same property?

Yes. They're separate schemes with separate budgets. A farmer can install a KUSUM-B solar pump on the farm AND a PM Surya Ghar rooftop solar system on the farmhouse independently. No double-counting because the systems serve different loads.

What if my farm is in Bundelkhand — do I get extra subsidy?

UP doesn't have an explicit Bundelkhand-bonus for KUSUM. However, Bundelkhand districts (Jhansi, Banda, Lalitpur, Mahoba, etc.) are typically prioritised in UPNEDA's quota allocation due to chronic drought. Your application is more likely to be approved faster from a Bundelkhand district than from a Lucknow tehsil with already-saturated quotas.

What's the difference between Component B and Component C?

Component B is for farmers without grid electricity — the solar pump is completely standalone, runs only on solar. Component C is for farmers with grid electricity — the solar adds capacity to your existing electric pump, you use solar power preferentially, and excess generation gets sold back to the DISCOM at ₹3.50/unit.

Component B suits drought-prone, off-grid areas (most of Bundelkhand, parts of Terai). Component C suits well-electrified districts where farmers want to reduce their existing pump bills.

How long does the subsidy take to come through?

The subsidy is not paid to the farmer directly. It goes to the vendor. The farmer only pays their 40% share. So you never "wait" for subsidy money — you pay your portion at vendor billing, and the subsidy is netted from the vendor's invoice automatically. Total timeline from application to operational pump: 90-120 days typically.

Can I get a bank loan for the 30% portion?

Yes. NABARD has channelled KUSUM loans through PNB, SBI, Bank of Baroda, Canara Bank, and most regional rural banks. Up to 30% of the total cost can be loan-financed at agricultural lending rates (typically 7-9% per annum, lower if you're a Kisan Credit Card holder). Loan tenure typically 5-7 years. You need to put up at least 10% as own funds — banks don't fund the entire farmer share.

What if my pump breaks down — who fixes it?

KUSUM pumps come with a mandatory 5-year comprehensive maintenance contract bundled with the installation. The vendor is contractually obligated to maintain the pump (free parts + labour) for 5 years from commissioning. This is one of KUSUM's strongest features compared to private-purchase solar pumps where the warranty is just 1-2 years.

Is the income from Component A (selling power to DISCOM) really ₹60K+ per acre per year?

That's the MNRE-calculated estimate for a 1 MW plant on 4 acres of land at UP's feed-in tariff of ₹3.07/unit. The exact number depends on solar irradiance at your location, plant capacity factor (typically 18-22% in UP), and tariff (which can rise modestly over the 25-year PPA period). Bundelkhand districts get the higher end (₹65-75K/acre) due to higher irradiance; eastern UP districts get the lower end (₹55-60K/acre).

One important caveat: you don't get this income directly. It's paid by the DISCOM (UPPCL) monthly to your bank account. Some UP DISCOMs have been slow to pay — Bundelkhand farmers have reported 60-90 day delays during 2024-25 due to UPPCL cash-flow issues. This is a real concern for Component A; we list it in eligibility-check warnings.
