§ Who Component A is built for

Best fit: landowner-farmers with idle land.

Component A is the most niche of the three KUSUM components — but for the right farmer, it's the most lucrative. The land has to meet specific criteria, the location has to work, and you have to be willing to lease the land out for 25 years.

✅ Strong fit if you have

  • 4-16 acres of barren, uncultivable, fallow, or pastoral land
  • The land is within 5 km of a 33/11 kV substation
  • Clear land ownership records (RoR / Khasra-Khatauni)
  • The land currently generates zero or minimal income
  • You're comfortable with a 25-year PPA arrangement
  • You can wait 12-18 months from application to operational plant

❌ Probably NOT for you if

  • Your land is cultivable and currently in productive use
  • Your land is more than 5 km from the nearest substation
  • Land ownership is disputed or unclear
  • The land is in a protected zone (forest, ecological)
  • You need income within the next 6-12 months
  • Less than 2 acres total

How the income reaches you

You don't operate the plant yourself. A solar developer (private EPC empanelled by MNRE/UPNEDA) builds and runs it. You receive income in one of two structures, depending on the deal you sign:

  • Lease model: Developer pays you fixed annual rent (₹50,000-80,000 per acre per year, paid by developer to your bank account quarterly). Lowest risk for you.
  • Revenue-share model: Developer pays you a percentage of monthly DISCOM revenue (typically 8-15%). Higher upside but tied to actual generation. Better if you're confident in irradiance + tariff.

UPNEDA mediates and approves both structures. The developer is required to submit the proposed payment structure to UPNEDA for verification before the PPA is signed.

⚠️ Real Caveat About UP DISCOM Payments

UPPCL (the main UP DISCOM) has had documented payment delays to Component A farmers in 2024-25 — 60 to 90 days behind schedule in some cases. This is due to UPPCL's broader financial situation, not the KUSUM scheme itself.

Before signing a 25-year PPA, ask your developer: What happens if UPPCL delays payment by 90+ days? A good developer will have a buffer in place. A bad developer will pass the delay to you.

§ Application process

From application to first payment.

Component A has the longest timeline of the three KUSUM components — typically 15-18 months from application to operational plant. Plan accordingly.

Step 01 · Month 1

Apply via Portal

Submit application on PM-KUSUM portal or via UPNEDA district office. Land documents + substation distance verification.

Step 02 · Month 2-3

UPNEDA Verification

Field inspection of land + electrical substation distance + grid capacity check by UPNEDA + DISCOM joint team.

Step 03 · Month 4-6

Developer Bidding

UPNEDA conducts reverse-auction with empanelled developers. You can choose your preferred bidder (within MNRE-approved list).

Step 04 · Month 6-8

Tripartite PPA

Sign 25-year PPA between you (farmer), developer, and DISCOM. UPNEDA witnesses + holds the agreement.

Step 05 · Month 9-18

Plant Construction

Developer builds plant, gets safety/electrical clearances, and commissions. Takes 8-12 months for 1 MW.

Step 06 · Month 18+

Monthly Income Starts

Commercial operation begins. DISCOM pays developer monthly; developer pays you per agreed structure. First payment within 30-60 days of commissioning.

§ Compare with other components

Component A vs B vs C.

If you have farmland but it's cultivable (not barren), Component A isn't a fit — but Component B or C might be:

  • Component B (Standalone Solar Pumps) → For cultivable land without grid electricity. You install a solar pump (1-10 HP) for irrigation. 60% subsidy. The most common KUSUM path for UP farmers.
  • Component C (Grid-Pump Solarisation) → For cultivable land WITH existing grid-connected electric pump. Adds solar to your existing pump. 60% subsidy + you can sell excess to DISCOM at ₹3.50/unit.

§ Component A FAQ for UP farmers

Common questions, direct answers.

Do I have to sell the land to the developer?

No — you retain ownership throughout the 25-year PPA. You're leasing the land usage rights. The land title stays in your name. After 25 years, the plant either renews the lease (negotiable terms) or is dismantled and the land returns to your unencumbered use.

What if I want to farm the land again before 25 years?

Once the PPA is signed and the plant is operational, you cannot reclaim the land for farming during the PPA period. This is a long-term commitment. If your circumstances change (inheritance, family needs, etc.), the agreement is transferable to your heirs but cannot be unilaterally terminated. Read the PPA terms carefully before signing.

How is land area requirement calculated?

Rough rule: 1 MW of solar capacity requires about 4 acres of usable land. So a 0.5 MW plant needs ~2 acres; 1 MW needs ~4 acres; 2 MW needs ~8 acres. The actual area depends on panel efficiency, layout, and inverter placement, but 4 acres per MW is a safe planning number for UP.

What does "within 5 km of substation" mean exactly?

The site must be within 5 km of a 33/11 kV electrical substation that has available evacuation capacity. Beyond 5 km, the transmission costs to connect the plant to the grid become economically unviable (the developer would lose money). UPNEDA verifies this distance during field inspection. If your land is 6-10 km away, you might still qualify if there's a nearer substation under construction.

Can I apply if my land is leased / mortgaged?

If your land has an active mortgage or loan against it, you'll need a No-Objection Certificate (NOC) from the lender before applying. Most NABARD-channelled banks issue these readily once they understand it's a revenue-generating use of the land. Leased land (where you don't own it) cannot be enrolled.

What's the difference between lease vs revenue-share models?

Lease: fixed annual rent (₹50K-80K/acre/year) paid regardless of generation. Predictable but caps your upside. Revenue-share: percentage of actual DISCOM revenue (8-15%). Variable but uncapped on the upside if irradiance is good. UPNEDA can mediate to ensure fairness. New farmers typically prefer the lease model for predictability; experienced ones go revenue-share for upside.

Can I apply as an FPO or cooperative?

Yes. Farmer Producer Organisations, cooperatives, gram panchayats, and water user associations are all eligible applicants. For FPOs with 50+ members, you get priority in UPNEDA's quota allocation. The application is filed in the FPO's name; revenue distribution among members is per your FPO bylaws.

Are there any tax implications?

Lease income from agricultural land is generally tax-exempt under Section 10(1) of the Income Tax Act. PPA revenue (where you're paid percentage of power sales) might fall under business income depending on your structure. Consult a CA before signing — the difference between "agricultural lease" and "business revenue" can mean a 20-30% tax difference over 25 years.

What happens if UPPCL stops paying?

The 25-year PPA is a tripartite agreement — UPPCL, the developer, and you. If UPPCL defaults, the developer has standing to sue. Most reputable developers carry payment-default insurance and have a 90-day cash buffer. Ask your developer for proof of these before signing. Without these safeguards, a UPPCL delay becomes your problem.

Can I install solar on the same land for my own farmhouse?

Yes — Component A and PM Surya Ghar (residential rooftop) are separate schemes with separate budgets. Your farmhouse can have rooftop solar under PM Surya Ghar while your barren land hosts a Component A plant. They serve different loads and don't conflict.
