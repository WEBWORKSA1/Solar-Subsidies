§ What it actually is

Component A in plain language.

You own agricultural land that's barren, uncultivable, fallow, or pastoral. Instead of letting it sit idle, you (or a developer with your consent) install a 0.5–2 MW solar power plant on it. The plant generates electricity, which is sold directly to your local DISCOM (in UP, that's UPPCL) under a 25-year Power Purchase Agreement at a regulator-set feed-in tariff of ₹3.07 per unit.

You earn a monthly payment from the DISCOM for the next 25 years. Your land remains yours. After 25 years, the plant is decommissioned or renewed under a fresh agreement.

Two ways to participate

  1. Self-developed: You raise the capital (₹3 Cr per MW), build the plant yourself, sell power. Highest returns but highest risk. Typically requires bank financing.
  2. Developer-led (more common): An MNRE-empanelled developer builds the plant on your land. You receive a fixed annual lease (typically ₹60–75K per acre per year, indexed to inflation). Zero capital from you. Lower returns but zero risk.
⚠️ Honest reality: Some UP DISCOMs (especially UPPCL) have been slow to pay Component A farmers in 2024-25, with 60-90 day delays reported. This affects both self-developed and developer-led models. Before signing any agreement, verify current UPPCL payment status with UPNEDA or talk to a farmer who has actually received Component A payouts in your district.
§ Eligibility

Who qualifies?

Component A has the most specific eligibility of all three KUSUM components. Most farmers don't qualify — not because they're disqualified, but because their land doesn't meet the criteria.

✅ You qualify if:

  • You own at least 4 acres of agricultural land
  • The land is classified as barren, uncultivable, fallow, or pastoral (not currently producing crops)
  • The land is within 5 km of a 33/11 kV electrical substation
  • You have clear ownership records (RoR / Khasra-Khatauni)
  • You're an individual farmer, FPO, panchayat, cooperative society, or water user association
  • The land isn't in a protected zone (no forest, no ecological reserve, no waterway buffer)

⚠️ You don't qualify if:

  • You have less than 4 acres available (Component B might fit instead — see below)
  • Your land is currently cultivated (diversion permissions are complex and slow)
  • You're more than 5 km from the nearest substation (transmission cost kills the economics)
  • Ownership is disputed or you're a tenant farmer
  • Your district has saturated Component A quota (Bundelkhand districts may have waitlists)
Less than 4 acres? Look at Component B (off-grid solar pumps) if you irrigate, or Component C if you have an existing electric pump. Both start from 0.5 acres.

What you'll earn (developer-led model).

Most common Component A scenario in UP: farmer owns 4 acres of barren land near a substation. An MNRE-empanelled developer (Adani, Tata Power Renewables, ReNew, etc) installs a 1 MW plant. Farmer gets lease payment for 25 years.

This is income from land that was previously producing zero. The developer takes installation risk + technology risk + DISCOM payment risk. You take none of that.

Variations by location

  • Bundelkhand districts (Jhansi, Banda, Lalitpur, Mahoba, Chitrakoot): Higher solar irradiance → ₹65–75K per acre per year
  • Western UP (Meerut, Saharanpur, Bareilly): Slightly lower irradiance → ₹55–65K per acre per year
  • Eastern UP (Varanasi, Gorakhpur, Prayagraj): Lowest irradiance → ₹50–60K per acre per year
§ The application process

From land assessment to monthly payouts.

Component A has the longest timeline of all KUSUM components — typically 18 months from initial application to commercial operation. The first 6 months are paperwork and approvals; the next 12 are construction.

Apply on PM-KUSUM portal or UPNEDA officeSubmit land records, Aadhaar, bank details. UPNEDA accepts applications in defined windows (typically 2–3 per year).
UPNEDA verification (45–60 days)Land ownership verified. Substation proximity confirmed (must be within 5 km of 33/11 kV). Site survey scheduled.
DISCOM (UPPCL) confirms PPA capacityUPPCL allocates evacuation capacity at the relevant substation. If saturated, you wait for the next window.
Developer selection or self-investment decisionUPNEDA matches you with an empanelled developer, or you self-fund. Negotiate lease terms (annual rate, indexation, exit clauses).
PPA signed (tri-party: farmer-developer-DISCOM)25-year Power Purchase Agreement signed. Lease agreement between you and developer. UPPCL guarantees offtake.
Construction (10–12 months)Developer constructs the plant. You retain land access during construction. Plant commissioned when grid sync is verified.
Commercial operation beginsUPPCL pays the developer monthly for generation. Developer pays your lease annually or quarterly per agreement.
§ Red flags + watchouts

What can go wrong.

🚩 Watch out for:

  • Developers demanding upfront fees: A legitimate Component A developer earns from the generation, not from you. Any "processing fee" or "deposit" demanded from you is a red flag.
  • Lease agreements shorter than 25 years: The PPA is 25 years. Your lease should match. Anything shorter and you risk the developer walking away with the plant after they've recouped capex.
  • Vague lease indexation: Inflation is real over 25 years. Your lease should index to CPI or another benchmark. Fixed ₹60K/acre forever loses ~50% real value over the contract.
  • No exit clauses: What if the developer defaults? What if UPPCL stops paying? Your lease should specify what happens. Get a lawyer.
  • Unclear panchayat consent (if applicable): If your land borders village commons, you may need panchayat NOC. Some developers skip this and farmers face local pushback during construction.
Specific to UP in 2025-26: UPPCL payment delays. Multiple Bundelkhand farmers reported 60–90 day payment delays in 2024-25 due to UPPCL cash-flow issues. Some developers have absorbed this by paying farmers from their own working capital; others have passed the delay through. Ask the developer explicitly: "What's your payment schedule to me if UPPCL pays you late?" Get the answer in writing.
