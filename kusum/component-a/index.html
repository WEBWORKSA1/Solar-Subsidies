§ What is Component A?

How Component A actually works.

Component A is structurally different from Components B and C. You're not buying a pump — you're leasing your barren land to host a solar power plant.

Here's the model: You own barren or uncultivable agricultural land near an electrical substation. Instead of leaving it idle, you partner with a developer (private EPC or RESCO — Renewable Energy Service Company) who builds a 0.5–2 MW solar plant on your land at their cost. They sign a 25-year Power Purchase Agreement (PPA) with UPPCL at ₹3.07/unit. You receive a monthly lease payment for the duration of the PPA.

The capex (₹2.5–3.5 crore per MW) is the developer's problem. The MNRE provides a generation-based incentive (GBI) of ₹0.40/unit to the developer for the first 5 years — that's the actual government subsidy. You, the farmer, pay nothing upfront. Your only contribution is the land.

✅ Why this is structurally good for farmers

Barren land in UP typically generates zero income. Component A converts that into ₹30,000–₹40,000 per acre per year (UPNEDA-published benchmark) for 25 years — guaranteed by PPA. After 25 years, the plant is decommissioned and land returns to you.

Compare to typical alternatives: leasing for grazing (₹2,000–5,000/acre/year), informal farming attempts (negative margin on barren land), or selling outright (one-time payment, no ongoing income).

§ Eligibility

Do you qualify?

✅ Strong fit

  • You own 4–16 acres of barren or non-cultivable land
  • Land is within 5 km of a 33/11 kV electrical substation
  • Land has clear ownership documents (RoR / Khasra-Khatauni)
  • You're an individual farmer, FPO, panchayat, cooperative society, or water user association
  • You're comfortable with a 25-year commitment (the land stays leased for 25 years)
  • Your land is not in a forest zone, ecological reserve, or disputed boundary area

⛔ Not a fit

  • Land is cultivable and currently producing crops (Component A requires uncultivable land)
  • Land is more than 5 km from a substation (transmission costs kill economics)
  • Land ownership is disputed, unclear, or in joint family arrangement without consent
  • You have less than 4 acres of barren land
  • You want short-term income (25-year lease is non-negotiable)
  • You're a tenant farmer without land ownership documents

Geographic variation matters. Bundelkhand (Banda, Jhansi, Lalitpur, Mahoba) gets higher irradiance — ₹65–75K per acre per year achievable. Eastern UP (Varanasi, Gorakhpur belt) gets lower — ₹55–60K range. Negotiate based on your specific location's solar profile.

What the farmer doesn't pay

  • Solar panel cost (developer pays — ~₹2 Cr/MW)
  • Inverter, mounting, cabling, civil works (~₹50L/MW)
  • Substation interconnection (~₹20–30L)
  • Panel cleaning, maintenance, security (developer's 25-year responsibility)
  • Insurance against theft, weather damage, equipment failure

⚠️ The honest caveat about UPPCL payment delays

The lease income is paid by the developer, who receives revenue from UPPCL. In 2024–25, UPPCL had cash-flow issues that caused 60–90 day payment delays to Component A farmers in some Bundelkhand districts. The developer typically still pays the farmer on time (it's their contractual obligation regardless of UPPCL timing), but worst-case, developers can delay your lease payment if UPPCL withholds revenue.

What to verify before signing: (1) Developer's financial strength — do they have working capital to bridge UPPCL delays? (2) Tripartite agreement that includes a UPPCL payment guarantee. (3) Late-payment penalty clause favoring the farmer.

§ The process

From application to monthly income.

Typical timeline: 15–18 months from initial application to commercial operation. Slower than Components B and C because of substation interconnection + PPA negotiation steps.

Step 01 · Month 1

Eligibility + land verification

Apply on UPNEDA KUSUM portal. Submit land records, Aadhaar, bank details. UPNEDA verifies land ownership and substation proximity.

Step 02 · Months 2-3

Developer matching

UPNEDA runs a reverse auction among MNRE-empanelled developers for your site. Developer with lowest tariff bid (or highest farmer payout) wins.

Step 03 · Months 4-5

PPA + lease signing

Tripartite agreement signed: you (landowner), developer, UPPCL. 25-year lease + 25-year PPA. Lease payments begin from commercial operation date.

Step 04 · Months 6-15

Construction + commissioning

Developer builds the plant (panels, inverters, transformers, substation interconnection). Site fenced, secured, commissioned. Bi-directional meter installed by UPPCL.

Step 05 · Month 15+

Commercial operation

Plant generates power. Your monthly lease payment begins, deposited directly into your bank account. Plant operates for 25 years, then decommissioned.

§ Red flags

What to watch for.

Component A involves long-term contracts with significant downside if structured badly. Walk away from any deal with these red flags:

  • Developer asks for upfront payment from you. Component A is supposed to be zero-cost for the farmer. Any "setup fee," "documentation fee," or "liaison fee" demand is a scam.
  • Lease term is less than 25 years. The MNRE scheme requires 25-year PPA. Shorter terms mean the developer is trying to optimize their economics at your expense.
  • No PPA signed with UPPCL at agreement time. Without a confirmed PPA, the developer has no revenue source — and you have no lease guarantee.
  • Developer is not MNRE-empanelled. Verify on https://mnre.gov.in. Empanelled developers have technical and financial capacity vetted by the ministry.
  • Vague clauses on payment delays or default. Demand specific terms: how often, what currency, late-payment penalty, dispute resolution mechanism.
  • Pressure tactics or rushed signing. A 25-year commitment deserves at least 30 days of legal review. Anyone rushing you is incentivized against your interest.
